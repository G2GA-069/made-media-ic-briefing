What the Grimleys Want

Structuring Levers

Vendor Loan (£500k, 5yr): The Grimleys need this to feel like real money. Propose 6% cash-pay (not PIK — we want alignment). 18-month interest-only, then amortising. Subordinated to bank. This keeps them engaged post-close and creates a natural transition incentive. The model's PIK-only structure at 6% is a gift to the buyer but sellers will push back.

Earn-out (£500k, 3 tranches): The model structures this as 3 annual payments tied to EBITDA milestones (£620k, £650k, £750k). This is reasonable. The milestones are achievable under the base case (FY26: £713k, FY27: £719k, FY28: £820k). A 10% surplus sharing mechanism is modelled — excess EBITDA above milestone generates a special dividend. This is seller-friendly but creates strong alignment.

Bank Debt (£750k, 1.5x): Leverageable. UK SME lenders (OakNorth, Shawbrook, HSBC Growth) will finance 1.0-2.0x for a profitable agency with recurring revenue. Interest at ~10% all-in (base + margin). 5-year term with 10% mandatory amortisation + cash sweep. Entry leverage coverage: EBITDA/interest = 500k/75k = 6.7x. Comfortable.

AI Disruption Risk Assessment

HIGH RISK — Agency Project Revenue

~£3.8m of revenue (project work + adhoc support) is exposed to AI code generation. Cursor/Claude/Copilot are compressing development timelines. Mid-market CMS builds that took 3 months will take 3 weeks within 2-3 years. The subcontractor-heavy model (£1.5m+ in direct costs at MMI alone) is the first thing AI replaces. Mitigant: Design, UX strategy, and client relationship management are harder to automate. The shift from "build" to "configure + advise" is manageable if management pivots.

LOW RISK — SaaS Products

CrowdHandler (virtual queue) and Basker (unclear, likely analytics/CRM for venues) are operational SaaS tools. AI doesn't replace "queue my visitors" or "manage my venue" — it might enhance them. CrowdHandler's 180 customers and 100% recurring revenue are structurally protected. Risk is more competitive than AI-driven.

§8 — Intercompany & Bridge Mechanics

Intercompany Loans — The Core Issue

CrowdHandler owes MML £168k. Basker owes MML £320k. Total: £488k. These are loans from the target (MML) to sister entities that the Grimleys control. Per the PDF, both entities are "now breaking even on revenue" and "CrowdHandler making repayments."

Per the system prompt framework: An interco receivable from a related-party entity is economically equivalent to a dividend the sellers paid themselves. The bridge deduction is simply reflecting that the cash balance is lower than it should be. If the receivable had been repaid and then paid out as a dividend, the result is identical. Show the interco explicitly for negotiation transparency, but do not treat it as a separate economic wrong from the cash extraction.

Preferred resolution: Cash repayment pre-close. CrowdHandler has £104k cash (Mar 26) — it can repay most of its £168k. Basker has £10k cash and negative net assets (£-38k) — it cannot repay its £320k. This means the Basker loan is effectively written off at completion. Under CTA 2009 s.354-358, no tax relief on the write-off between connected companies. Assume zero benefit; any crystallised DTA is post-close upside.

Is There a Double-Count with Lease Normalisation?

Currently unknown — we need the lease terms. If MML is renting office space from a Grimley-related entity below market, the interco deduction (balance sheet hole / stock) and the lease normalisation (P&L suppression / flow / capitalised perpetuity) are measuring different things. Both are defensible. Sellers will argue double-count; the response is prepared per the framework above.

§11 — Preliminary Investment Thesis

Bull case: Acquire a profitable, cash-generative UK digital agency at 5x EBITDA with two embedded SaaS options (CrowdHandler growing 19% YoY, Basker at inflection point). BlocksOffice creates real switching costs. The cultural/heritage niche is defensible and under-penetrated in the US. Post-acquisition, reduce subcontractor dependency via AI tooling, push recurring revenue mix from ~45% to 60%+, and potentially spin out CrowdHandler as a separate SaaS exit at 5-8x ARR in 3-5 years. Total equity cheque: ~£700k for a business generating £500k+ EBITDA.

Bear case: Founder dependency is extreme (Jake is CEO + key client relationships). AI disruption compresses project margins within 2-3 years. The US entity is operationally a recharge vehicle with only 6 heads — all intellectual capital may reside in Jake's network. FY24's EBITDA collapse to £275k shows earnings volatility. The interco structure is messy. CrowdHandler and Basker are cash-consuming distractions from the core agency.

Base case conviction: The deal works if we can get in at 4.5-5.0x on a defensible normalised EBITDA of £500-550k, structure £500k as a vendor loan to keep the Grimleys aligned through transition, and use the earn-out to bridge any valuation gap. The SaaS assets are free upside at this price. Key DD workstream is client concentration and founder dependency.
